The Difference Between Line Editing & Proofreading (And Why It Matters)

By Avery Jensen May 2026 5 min read

"Do I really need both?" It's the question I hear more than almost any other — and it's a fair one. Line editing and proofreading sound like they overlap, so paying for both can feel like paying twice for the same thing. They don't overlap. They sit at opposite ends of the editing journey, and confusing them is one of the easiest ways to spend money on the wrong help at the wrong time.

Here's the simplest way I can put it: line editing makes your sentences better. Proofreading makes sure your sentences are correct. One is about craft and voice. The other is about catching the final mistakes before your reader ever sees them. You can't proofread your way to better prose, and you shouldn't be reworking sentences when you're supposed to be doing a final polish.

Let me break down what each one actually does.

What line editing actually covers

Line editing happens at the level of the sentence and the paragraph. I'm reading your prose closely — not for typos, but for how it works. Does this sentence carry its weight? Is the rhythm doing what you want? Are you telling me a character is anxious when the sentence could simply make me feel it?

When I line edit, I'm paying attention to:

  • Flow and rhythm — how sentences move into one another, and whether the pacing matches the moment.
  • Word choice — swapping the vague word for the precise one, trimming the ones that aren't earning their place.
  • Clarity — untangling sentences that make a reader stop and re-read.
  • Tone and voice — strengthening what's already yours, never replacing it with mine.
  • Repetition and crutch words — the phrases you lean on without realizing it.

Line editing is creative, collaborative work. It's the stage where your writing goes from "good" to "unmistakably yours, only sharper." It is not a final step — there's still going to be back-and-forth, revision, and new sentences written. Which is exactly why proofreading comes later.

What proofreading actually covers

Proofreading is the last set of eyes before your manuscript goes out into the world. By this point the writing is locked. I'm not rephrasing anything or questioning your pacing — I'm hunting for the small, objective errors that slip past everyone, including the author who has read the page forty times.

When I proofread, I'm catching:

  • Typos and spelling — the "form" that should be "from."
  • Punctuation — the missing comma, the rogue apostrophe.
  • Grammar slips — subject-verb disagreements, tense wobbles.
  • Consistency — is it "grey" or "gray," is a character's name spelled the same way throughout.
  • Formatting — spacing, indentation, stray double spaces, heading consistency.

Proofreading is precise, technical, and final. Done well, it's invisible — the reader never notices it, because there's nothing left to trip over.

Line Editing

  • Improves how you say it
  • Sentence flow & rhythm
  • Word choice & clarity
  • Voice & tone
  • Creative & collaborative
  • Comes before the final draft

Proofreading

  • Confirms it's correct
  • Typos & spelling
  • Punctuation & grammar
  • Consistency & formatting
  • Technical & final
  • Comes last, before publishing
"Line editing is the rehearsal where the performance finds its shape. Proofreading is the moment just before the curtain rises, making sure nothing's out of place."

So — do you need both?

For most manuscripts headed to publication, yes. They do different jobs at different stages, and skipping either one shows. A book that's been beautifully line edited but never proofread can still embarrass an author with a typo on page three. A book that's been proofread but never line edited might be technically flawless and still read flat.

But you don't need both at the same time, and you may not need both right now. Here's the order I'd suggest thinking about it:

  • Still shaping the big picture — plot, characters, structure? You're at the developmental editing stage, before either of these.
  • Story's solid but the prose feels clunky or uneven? You need a line edit.
  • Everything's final, polished, and you're nearly ready to publish? Time for a proofread.
A quick gut check If you're still planning to change sentences, it's too early to proofread — you'd just be re-introducing errors after the polish. Line edit first, lock the prose, then proofread last.

The bottom line

Line editing and proofreading aren't competing services — they're partners that happen to live at opposite ends of the same journey. Knowing the difference means you spend your editing budget where it'll actually help, in the order that makes sense. And if you're not sure which stage you're at, that's exactly the kind of thing worth a quick conversation before you commit to anything.
